1 - Grass fields

I prefer using the ‘empty’ grass tile the most, and the ‘detail’ grass tiles sparingly.

Avoid placing the same detail tile repeatedly nearby in a row or column to reduce obvious patterns. Clumping details together in different configurations also helps.

Shorter grass, green field. These tiles are a complete 2-corner Wang Tileset.

For more information on Wang Tilesets, go here: http://www.cr31.co.uk/stagecast/wang/2corn.html

The four tiles on the right are alternate centre tiles. Use sparingly to avoid obvious repetition. Of these tiles, the tile in the upper right is the most forgiving. I find it can be used more often without it being obvious.

Hill tiles. These tiles can be used together (much like a Wang Tileset) or individually. Break up long rows or columns of hillside tiles with empty grass field tiles to avoid repetition. Concave hill corners aren’t included as empty grass field tiles work just fine.

Also works well alongside the cliff tiles.

5 - Mountains

There are many combinations you can use these tiles in. Here’s a quick method for putting mountains together:

  1. Place down placeholder tiles for lit and shadow sides of the mountain range.
    To avoid making a configuration that isn’t possible with the tiles, stick roughly to north-to-south mountain ranges (there aren’t any horizontal ridge tiles).
  2. Mountain edge tiles.
  3. Mountain ridge tiles. The diagonal ridge tiles can be used to break up a long vertical section.
  4. Alternate mountainside tiles. Using the lighter tiles near the mountain ridge helps sell the height of the mountain.

These tiles are for mountain ridges that are inside another mountain range or joined up in some fashion.

8 - Ocean (upper)

These tiles are a complete 2-corner Wang Tileset.

For more information on Wang Tilesets, go here: http://www.cr31.co.uk/stagecast/wang/2corn.html

Ping-pong style animation, 1-2-3-4-3-2. Framespeed in example is 240ms.

1st row: standard ocean-grass field edge.

2nd row: standard ocean-cliff edge.

3rd row: shallow ocean-beach edge.

The water is transparent, and the ocean (under) tiles should be placed on a lower layer.

1st row (ocean-grass): use standard ocean tiles.

Can transition straight into deep ocean with appropriate tile underneath.

3rd row: use shallow ocean tiles.

Can transition straight into standard ocean with appropriate tile underneath.

Because the cliff-ocean tiles don’t play so nicely with the transition tiles, they don’t have this feature.

If your game engine has a limited amount of layers or you don’t need this feature, there is a seperate file, oceantiles_notransparency.png, that has flattened the ocean layers.

9 - Ocean edge transition tiles

These tiles use the same 1-2-3-4-3-2 ping-pong style animation as the ocean. Example framespeed at 240 ms.

For the beach transition tiles, underneath you can use many of the shallow-standard ocean (under) transition tiles.

10 - Beach tiles

These beach tiles transition into the standard grass tiles. They don’t require inside concave corners, so aren’t a complete Wang set. The reason for this is so that you can squeeze them in close to the beach shoreline tiles. Instead, I’ve made some alternate corner tiles so that you can create smooth curves like in the example.

Beach cliff tiles are the same as the regular cliff tiles (2-corner Wang Tileset) Only addition are the beachcliff-grasscliff transition tiles.

11 - Ocean (under) tiles

1-2-3-4-3-2 ping-pong animation. 240 ms per frame in example. Complete 2-corner Wang Tileset, plus extra 2x2 tile sized corner/curve pieces for smoother curves.

Using only 2-corner Wang Tileset.

Using 2x2 corner/curve pieces to smooth curves.

Since the 2x2 corner/curve tiles take up a lot of space in the tileset, you can certainly live without them if necessary.

They can also be reduced to the following size if your engine can rotate tiles:

oceantiles_reduced.png

You can halve that further if you’re able to palette swap tiles, since the shallow water and deep water sets are identical except for colour change.

For an extra touch of detail to your ocean maps, use these sparkles.

Using too many becomes distracting, and they look best on shallow waters.
